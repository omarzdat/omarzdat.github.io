Why Do Baby's Movements Matter?

Your baby's only direct link to the outside world is through you. Movement is their language‚Äîtheir way of telling you they're thriving.

Taking time each day to monitor your baby's movements and getting to know who your baby is can be one of the most meaningful parts of pregnancy. By setting aside quiet moments to feel your baby kick, roll, or poke, you're not just bonding with your bump‚Äîyou're also checking on your baby's health and wellbeing.

1
Every baby is unique. Even if you've been pregnant before, each baby and pregnancy is different. Avoid comparing your experience to others‚Äîfocus on getting to know this baby's individual personality and patterns.
2
There's no magic number. Forget counting to 10 kicks. Your baby will develop their own pattern of movements. A movement can be anything‚Äîa kick, flutter, poke, or roll. You'll typically start feeling these between 16 and 24 weeks.
3
Movements don't slow down. The type of movement may change as your baby grows, but once you've established a routine, the strength, pattern, and frequency should remain consistent right up until birth.

What Happens When You Call with Concerns?

Reduced fetal movements‚Äîor a rapid increase‚Äîcan be a sign that your baby is unwell and needs investigation. Here's what should happen when you contact your healthcare provider:

1. Immediate Response
Your doctor, maternity ward, or midwife should ask you to come in right away. Don't accept "wait and see" advice when it comes to changed fetal movements.
2. Monitoring & Assessment
They will monitor your baby's heartbeat using electronic fetal monitoring (CTG) or conduct an ultrasound to check on your baby's wellbeing, movement, and fluid levels.
3. Your Right to Further Care
If you still have concerns even after being reassured that all is normal, you have the right to further investigation. Trust your instinct and insist on additional action.
4. Don't Leave Until Satisfied
It can simply be a feeling of unease or a sinking feeling that something isn't right. Do not leave the hospital until you're completely satisfied with the care you've received.

Safe Sleep in Pregnancy

Sleeping on your side during pregnancy has the potential to reduce late-term stillbirth. When you're pregnant, side sleeping helps maximize blood flow and oxygen to your growing baby.

From 28 weeks onward, going to sleep on your side‚Äîeither side‚Äîcan help keep your baby safer.

[Image: Pregnant Woman Sleeping on Side]

Why Does Sleep Position Matter?

Research shows that from about 28 weeks pregnant, you should start going to sleep on your side. Either side is fine‚Äîthe key is avoiding sleeping flat on your back for prolonged periods.

If you wake up on your back in the middle of the night, don't worry‚Äîjust roll over onto your side again. Falling asleep on your side means you stay in that position during the deepest and often longest part of your sleep. That's why we emphasize "settling" to sleep on your side.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which side should I sleep on?
‚ñº
Either side is perfectly fine! The most important thing is that you're not sleeping flat on your back for extended periods. Left or right side‚Äîwhichever is more comfortable for you‚Äîwill help maintain optimal blood flow to your baby. Some women find it helpful to alternate sides throughout the night.
When should I start sleeping on my side?
‚ñº
You should start practicing side sleeping from 28 weeks of pregnancy onward. If you naturally sleep on your side already, that's great! If you're typically a back or stomach sleeper, start transitioning to side sleeping around this time. Using pillows to support your bump and between your knees can make side sleeping much more comfortable.
What if I'm uncomfortable or have injuries?
‚ñº
If you're experiencing discomfort or have existing injuries that make side sleeping difficult, speak with your healthcare provider. They can recommend pregnancy pillows, positioning aids, or physical therapy to help make side sleeping more manageable. Don't suffer in silence‚Äîthere are solutions available to help you sleep safely and comfortably. Your provider may also suggest specific positioning modifications based on your individual circumstances.
üí§ Don't Panic If You Wake Up On Your Back

Waking up on your back occasionally is completely normal and not a cause for alarm. Simply roll onto your side and go back to sleep. The important thing is that you're falling asleep on your side, which means you're spending the longest, deepest part of your sleep in the safest position.
