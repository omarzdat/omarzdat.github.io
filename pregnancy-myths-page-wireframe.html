1

"If your baby's movements slow down, drink cold ice-water to get them moving again."

FALSE - DANGEROUS MYTH

This is 100% not true. If you ever present a change in your baby's movements and are told to go home and drink cold water, disregard this advice immediately and DO NOT leave your clinic until you are reviewed properly.

The Truth:
Any change in your baby's movement patterns requires immediate medical assessment. Decreased fetal movement can be a warning sign that your baby needs attention. Never try home remedies to stimulate movement - contact your healthcare provider right away.
×
2

"If your placenta is at the front you won't be able to feel your baby's movements."

FALSE

While it's true that an anterior placenta (placenta at the front) can cushion some movements, you should still be able to feel your baby move regularly.

The Truth:
An anterior placenta may make movements feel different or more subtle, but you will still establish a pattern unique to your baby. If you're not feeling regular movements, contact your healthcare provider regardless of placenta position.
×
3

"My baby will 'slow down' before being born as it will 'run out of room to move'."

FALSE

This is one of the most dangerous myths in pregnancy. While the type of movements may change as your baby grows, the frequency should remain consistent.

The Truth:
Your baby should maintain their individual pattern of movement right up until birth. Movements may feel different (more rolling than kicks) as baby grows, but they should not decrease. A reduction in fetal movement requires immediate medical attention.
×
4

"I should aim to count 10 kicks a day, right?"

OUTDATED ADVICE

Kick counting is outdated advice that focuses on a fixed number rather than your baby's individual pattern.

The Truth:
Instead of counting to 10, focus on knowing your baby's normal pattern of movement. Every baby is different - some are more active than others. What matters is noticing any change from what is normal for YOUR baby. If movements decrease or change significantly, contact your healthcare provider immediately.

5

"When my baby is 'going crazy' this is good, because any increase in movements is good!"

FALSE

While movement is good, excessive or frantic movements that are out of character for your baby can sometimes indicate distress.

The Truth:
Erratic, intense, or "crazy" movements that are very different from your baby's normal pattern should be checked by your healthcare provider. Trust your instincts - if something feels different or concerning, it's always better to get it checked.
×
6

Women should avoid sex during pregnancy.

FALSE

For most healthy pregnancies, sex is perfectly safe and will not harm your baby or cause early labor.

The Truth:
Unless your healthcare provider has given you specific reasons to avoid intercourse (such as placenta previa or risk of preterm labor), sex during pregnancy is safe. Your baby is well protected by the amniotic sac and uterine muscles. Always discuss any concerns with your healthcare provider.
